Effects of chosen chemokines on neuronal properties and synaptic transmission in central and basolateral complex of the rat amygdala

6 spines

In concert with electrophysiological data, the alterations in dendritic morphology was observed in the primary neuronal cultures of the amygdala. Dendritic spines are small dendritic protrusions that receive most (>90%) excitatory glutamatergic inputs (Rochefort and Konnerth, 2012; Yuste, 2013; Chen et al., 2014), while GABAergic terminals contact only a low proportion of them (Kubota et al., 2007; Brusco et al., 2014, Müllner et al., 2015). A large body of evidence supports the critical role of dendritic spines and their morphology in neuronal excitability and synaptic connectivity (Hering & Sheng, 2001; Kasai et al., 2003; Harms & Dunaevsky, 2007). Hence, observed changes in dendritic spine morphology further indicate a significant impact of CX3CL1 acute application on BLA physiology. Indeed, spines with large, prominent heads and short necks are generally thought to be stable, express large numbers of AMPA-type glutamate receptors, and form stronger synaptic connections than thin spines (Harris et al., 1992; Kasai et al., 2003; Ganeshina et al., 2004). By contrast, spines with small heads and filopodial protrusions were reported to be motile and unstable, and contribute to weak or silent synaptic connections. This type of dendritic spine morphology dominates during development and is considered as immature. Here, cultured primary neurons from the amygdala showed a trend toward wider dendritic heads upon CX3CL1(5nM) application. Intriguingly, when neurons were cultured in the additional presence of AraC, which was shown to decrease the number of glial cells in culture without altering the neurons viability [], the CX3CL1(5nM) application led to decreased length and width of dendritic spines. Notably, no difference between these parameters was observed between control and AraC-supplemented medium, illustrating that AraC treatment did not affect dendritic morphology. Thus, it is possible that these unexpected findings might reflect the CX3CL1-mediated neuron-glial cross-talk. We hypothesize that during “normal” condition, the CX3CL1 treatment activated CX3CR1 receptors on both, amygdalar neurons and the remaining glial fraction (5%), leading to observed CX3CL1-modulated wider dendritic spines. Our immunostaining data showed that the CX3CR1 receptor was expressed on both neurons and astrocytes, further supporting this hypothesis. Given that glia, especially microglia, are crucial players in the dendritic maturation, together with microglial abundance of CX3CR1 expression, it is plausible that this trend could be more prominent when glial component is higher.

Moreover, under conditions of more decreased number of surrounding glial cells, the CX3CL1-mediated neuron-glial communication is more limited and the activation of CX3CR1 on glial cells further reduced and under those conditions CX3CL1-mediated effects led to observed shorter and thinner dendritic spines. In support of this supposition, Basilico et al. (2019) found that CX3CR1-/- transgenic mice displayed a higher number of silent synapses in the hippocampus in comparison to their wild-type littermates. Those synapses were characterized by an immature AMPA/NMDA ratio with defective AMPA components and decreased release probability. Noting that this knockout strain is characterized by the absence of CX3CR1 only in microglia (but the CX3CR1 expression is not observed anywhere else), these observations implicate, similar to our findings, that the lack of microglial CX3CR1 activation, either due to transgenic deletion or by reduced number of those cells, results in immature phenotype of dendritic spines. 6.1 CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling in the basolateral amygdala

Herein, although the CXCL12/CXCR4 pair did not significantly affect principal cells’ excitability, excitatory, or inhibitory synaptic transmission, some interesting tendencies were noted. In particular, results presented here indicated that following CXCL12 treatment: (i) there was a trend toward increased excitability of principal cells (especially at higher concentration), (ii) there was a complex pattern of neuronal responses manifested as frequency of sEPSC, and (iii) there were opposing trends of CXCL12-mediated regulation of GABAergic synaptic transmission depending on the chemokine concentration.

CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling was previously studied in the basolateral nucleus of the amygdala in the context of neuroinflammation (Yang et al., 2016). Nevertheless, this report demonstrated neuronal expression of CXCR4 receptor as well as the CXCL12-induced CXCR4-dependent enhancement in the excitatory synaptic transmission in the BLA. The authors further suggested that the latter effect might result in the increased excitability of pyramidal-like neurons. Their hypothesis is supported by results presented in this PhD dissertation. However, the results regarding excitatory synaptic transmission in the BLA have been less straightforward. In particular, the study by Yang et al. (2016) revealed that the application of CXCL12 at the dose of 2nM enhanced glutamatergic transmission by increasing sEPSC frequency in the BLA. In contrast, results presented here showed that CXCL12(2nM) led to the enhancement of sEPSC frequency in 2 out of 12 neurons, while reducing this parameter in 5 out of 12 cells (Fig. 4.12a and b1). Thus, grouped data showed no statistically significant change in the frequency of sEPSCs. These neuronal proportions were also similar when higher concentration of exogenous CXCL12, i.e., 5nM, was applied (Fig.4.12a and b2). This discrepancy between studies may be explained by different experimental settings. Firstly, the whole-cell patch-clamp experiments performed by Yang et al. (2016) in C57BL/6 male mice, whereas this study was performed using slices obtained from male Wistar rats. The difference between those animals were reported in many contexts. Moreover, while in the present studies the coronal slices were used, transverse slices containing the amygdala were reported to be used in the study by Yang et al. (2016). Accordingly, the terminals from hippocampus and cortex, two sources of excitatory inputs into the BLA, are more intact in transverse slices. This raises the possibility that the observed CXCL12-induced enhancement in transverse slices may be, at least in part, due to additional hippocampal and/or cortical inputs, which are absent in coronal slices. The temperature, which is the critical factor for recording the synaptic transmission, also differs between these two studies. Here, brain slices were submerged with the ACSF at the temperature of 31–33°C, whereas the excitatory synaptic transmission in experiments by Yang et al. (2016) was recorded at room temperature. Finally, while present experiments were performed using normal ACSF, Yang et al. (2016) sEPSC recordings were made in the presence of a blocker of GABAA receptors, picrotoxin. Therefore, these findings raise the possibility that the enhancement in excitatory synaptic transmission onto principal cells in the BLA seen in Yang et al. (2016) study, here was distorted by the modulation of GABAergic heterogeneous group of local interneurons. Given the well-known tight regulation of principal cell’s activity by local interneurons, along with documented CXCL12 mediation of GABAergic transmission, this explanation is very likely. Nevertheless, whether blocking GABAA receptors will result in a significant increase of sEPSC frequency onto principal cells in the BLA and/or clearly in enhancement of their firing in rat coronal acute slices remains to be confirmed.

6.2 CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling in the lateral subdivision of the central amygdala

This dissertation hereby provides new evidence for the neuromodulatory properties of CXCL12/CXCR4 axis in synaptic transmission and membrane properties in the lateral subdivision of the central complex of the amygdala. Firstly, CXCL12 was shown to upregulate the excitability of CeL neurons, which was mediated by the CXCR4 receptor, but probably without astrocytic involvement. Next, there was a cell-specific trend illustrating the opposing CXCL12-mediated modulation of glutamatergic synaptic transmission based on the concentration, specifically onto Regular-Spiking neurons. Finally, the treatment with CXCL12 also led to opposite regulation of GABAergic transmission, based on the subtype of CeL neurons, which was possibly dependent on astrocytes.

6.2.1 Excitability

CXCL12 was shown to increase the excitability of CeL neurons. While Late-Firing neurons were shown to have both decreased threshold current and gain, only threshold current was decreased in the Regular-Firing neurons. These effects were mediated by CXCR4 receptor, as the presence of AMD3100 prevented this enhancement in CeL neuronal excitability. Interestingly, AMD3100 alone was shown in an opposite trend. Thus, these findings suggest tonic activity of CXCR4.

Moreover, the pre-incubation slices with astrocytic toxin, fluorocitrate, did not abolish CXCL12-triggered increase in neuronal excitability, suggesting that astrocytes did not mediate this effect. This is in line with our immunofluorescent data demonstrating the absence of CXCR4 in astrocytes. Similarly, other studies reported the CXCL12-mediated neuromodulation accompanied by the lack of astrocytic CXCR4 in the BLA and in …..

6.2.4 astrocytes

Moreover, astrocytes may regulate synaptic transmission by modulating the expression and protein levels of glutamate transporters in the membrane and thus control glutamate spillover beyond the synapse, which may reflect a change in the kinetics in EPSCs, which translate into synaptic plasticity (Murphy-Royal et al., 2015).

In relation to astrocytic regulation of synaptic transmission, evidence illustrated that based on frequency and duration of interneuron firing, astrocytes could tune in to release either glutamate, which enhances excitatory synaptic activity (Perea et al., 2016), or ATP/adenosine, which decreases excitatory synaptic strength (Covelo & Araque, 2018).

Moreover, recent studies have begun to elucidate that connexins, the gap junction forming proteins highly expressed on astrocytes, scale synaptic transmission, and plasticity. For instance, while Cx43, the most abundant astrocytic connexin, does not appear to regulate astrocytic membrane properties, there is increasing evidence that it forms hemichannels and regulates the excitatory synaptic transmission of hippocampal CA1 pyramidal cells via ATP signaling (Chever et al., 2014). Moreover, further experiments demonstrated that Cx43 reduced the amplitude of NMDA-mediated EPSCs in the prefrontal cortex. This effect could be rescued with the presence of D-serine in the extracellular medium (Meunier et al., 2017).

It is worth noting that recently ACKR3, an atypical receptor of CXC12, was suggested to internalize Cx43 (Meyrath et al., 2020). Therefore it is tempting to speculate that ACKR3-induced Cx43 internalization in the hippocampus or prefrontal cortex would lead to dysfunction in synaptic transmission and, in turn, functional consequences on learning and memory. However, to our knowledge, there are no data that would confirm this hypothesis.
